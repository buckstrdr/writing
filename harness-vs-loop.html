Harness Engineering vs Loop Engineering: What's Actually the Difference?

I learned the difference the hard way — overnight, while I was asleep. Here's the version that saves you the morning I had.

buckstrdr · 7 min read

I left an agent running overnight once. By morning it had opened six pull requests, merged three of them, deleted a pile of "unused" helper functions, and renamed half the files in the repo. Then it ticked the task off as complete and, I can only assume, felt rather good about itself.

Everything was green. Every test passed. And the codebase was measurably worse than when I'd gone to bed — two of those "unused" helpers were very much in use, a chunk of my pipeline had quietly broken, and I spent most of the next morning unpicking what my tireless little employee had done while I slept.

That wasn't the model being stupid. GPT didn't get dumber overnight. That was me looping before I had a harness — and it's the entire reason the difference between those two words is worth ten minutes of your time.

Because right now you'll see both terms thrown around like they're the same thing, or like you have to pick a side. Neither's true. One sits on top of the other, and if you get the order wrong, you get a morning like mine.

The harness is everything around the model

After that night, I started caring a lot more about the harness than about which model I was running. The model's a commodity — a good one is a download away. The harness is the stuff that makes it trustworthy: the tools it can reach, the memory it keeps between runs, the isolation that stops one agent trampling another's work, and — the bit I'd clearly skipped — the checks that catch it when it's confidently wrong.

It's not a fringe idea. Mitchell Hashimoto (yes, the Terraform one) gave it the name; OpenAI's Ryan Lopopolo wrote it up properly after a three-person team shipped over a million lines of production code with no human typing a single line of it. Codex wrote the lot — 1,500 merged PRs. That doesn't happen because the model's a genius. It happens because the harness around it got good enough to leave alone. Which, clearly, mine wasn't.

Loop engineering is the question I'd skipped

Loop engineering isn't "how do I make the model reliable." It's "how do I get out of the chair completely." Which, on paper, is exactly what I wanted at 11pm — kick it off, go to bed, wake up to finished work.

Avi Chawla frames it well: in a hand-run session you're doing two jobs — deciding what the agent does next, and checking its output before the next step. Loop engineering shoves both into the system. A schedule decides what runs, a maker agent does the work, a separate checker grades it, a file on disk holds the state, and it loops until it's done or hits a limit.

See the word I keep landing on? Separate checker. That's the one rule you cannot skip — the thing that decides the work is good can't be the thing that made it. My overnight agent was its own checker. It graded its own homework at 3am and gave itself an A, and I woke up to the consequences.

Why it's easy to confuse the two

They share a lot of furniture, which is why I'd blurred them together in the first place. Both start from the same heresy — the scaffolding matters more than the model. Both use a maker and a checker. Both want isolated worktrees, memory that outlives the context window, real tools wired in. List the parts of a loop and half of them are just harness bits with a different label. So on the surface they look like one discipline wearing two hats.

The actual difference

They're not, though — and the difference is exactly what I got wrong. A harness is about structure: it makes each individual step trustworthy, and its question is "can I trust what just happened?" A loop is about autonomy: it makes the whole thing run without you, and its question is "can I walk away?"

Here's the tell, and it's the thing I'd have paid good money to understand before that night: you can build a harness and never loop it. That's just careful coding with your hand on the wheel — completely valid, no shame in it. But you cannot loop safely without a harness underneath. The loop is a layer you earn the right to add once you trust the machine below it. I hadn't earned it. I looped anyway. Hence the morning.

A loop amplifies whatever you already have

This is the bit that actually explains my six PRs. A loop doesn't fix a weak harness — it multiplies it. My checks were rubbish, so instead of merging one bad PR the loop merrily merged three while I dreamed. If I'd had proper checks and real isolation, that exact same loop would've been fine — probably even useful. The loop didn't cause the damage; it just did what a weak harness told it to, over and over, faster than I could notice. Autonomy without a harness isn't "autonomous." It's "unsupervised."

So, the order of operations I use now

Build the harness first. Get to where a single step is trustworthy — real checks, isolation, memory. Then ask one question: is "done" something a machine can actually verify? If it is — tests pass, lint clean, the boring stuff — loop it and go make tea. If "done" needs my judgment, my hand stays on it.

I now loop the boring, verifiable things happily. And I would never again, as long as I live, leave something that can rename files at 3am running unwatched.

It's a stack, not a fight

Prompt engineering was the words. Context engineering was what the model can see. Harness engineering is the environment it works in. Loop engineering is taking your hands off. Each one sits on the last — and if you skip a rung, you find out the hard way, usually before breakfast.

The people quietly shipping with this stuff didn't pick loop over harness. They built a harness good enough that looping it wasn't a horror story. The model was never the moat. It's everything you build around it — and then, once you trust that, everything you build around that.
