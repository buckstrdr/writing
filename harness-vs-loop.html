Harness Engineering vs Loop Engineering: What's Actually the Difference?

Everyone's using the two terms like they mean the same thing. They don't — but they're not rivals either. Here's how they actually fit together.

buckstrdr · 7 min read

Open X right now and you'll trip over both terms inside about thirty seconds. "Harness engineering is the new moat." "Loop engineering is how you remove yourself as the bottleneck." Half the posts use them like synonyms; the other half act like you have to pick a side.

Both are wrong. They're not the same thing, and they're not competitors — one sits on top of the other. Once that clicks, most of the noise sorts itself out. So let me untangle it.

What harness engineering actually is

The harness is everything around the model. Not the model itself — the model's basically a commodity now, and a good one is a download away. The harness is the scaffolding that turns raw intelligence into something you'd actually trust: the tools it can reach, the memory it keeps between runs, the isolation that stops one agent trampling another's work, the checks that catch it when it's confidently wrong, the observability so you can see what it actually did.

The term got its legs from Mitchell Hashimoto (yes, the Terraform bloke), and then a proper writeup from OpenAI's Ryan Lopopolo in early 2026. And it wasn't theory — it came off the back of a three-person team that shipped over a million lines of production code without a human typing a single one of them. Codex wrote all of it. 1,500 merged PRs. That doesn't happen because the model got smarter overnight. It happens because the harness around it got good enough to trust.

What loop engineering actually is

Loop engineering is answering a different question. It's not "how do I make the model reliable" — it's "how do I get out of the chair entirely."

Avi Chawla framed it well: in a hand-run session you're doing two jobs — deciding what the agent does next, and checking its output before the next step. Loop engineering shoves both of those into the system. A schedule decides what runs. A maker agent does the work. A separate checker grades it. A file on disk holds the state between runs. It loops until it's done, or hits a max number of tries, or burns through its budget — then it stops.

The non-negotiable bit — and honestly this is the whole game — is the maker/checker split. Whatever decides the work is good cannot be the thing that made it. A model asked to grade its own output will almost always wave it through; a loop with no outside check is just an agent nodding at its own homework, forever. So the checker has to be something else: a hard signal like a failing test or a type error, or a second model running different instructions whose only job is to find the fault.

Why everyone conflates them

The confusion is fair enough, because they share a lot of furniture. Both start from the same heresy: the scaffolding matters more than the model. Both lean on a maker/checker separation. Both want isolated worktrees so agents don't clobber each other, memory that outlives the context window, real tools wired in properly. If you list the components of a loop, half of them are just harness primitives with a different label on the box.

So on the surface they look like one discipline with two names. They're not.

Where they genuinely differ

The difference is what each one is actually for.

Harness engineering is about structure. It makes each individual step reliable. Its unit is the action — one tool call, one edit, one review — and its question is: "can I trust what just happened?"

Loop engineering is about autonomy. It makes the whole thing run without you. Its unit is the session — the entire run from trigger to done — and its question is: "can I walk away?"

Put crudely: the harness is the machine. Loop engineering is pressing 'go' and wandering off to make a cup of tea. And here's the tell that one sits on top of the other — you can build a harness and never loop it. That's just careful interactive coding, human in the chair, and it's completely valid. But you cannot build a good loop without a harness underneath it. The loop is a layer you bolt on after you trust the harness.

Why a good loop needs a good harness

This is the part people skip, and then get bitten by. A loop doesn't fix a flaky harness — it amplifies it. Whatever your harness does, good or bad, the loop now does a hundred times while you're not watching.

If your checker is weak, the loop cheerfully merges rubbish faster than anyone can read it, and three weeks later nobody understands their own codebase. If your agents aren't isolated, the loop turns the occasional collision into a constant one. The loop is a force multiplier, and it multiplies whatever's already there — which is exactly why the boring harness properties (verification you can actually trust, isolation, state that survives a restart) are what let you leave the loop alone in the first place. Take those away and "autonomous" just means "unsupervised."

So when do you reach for which?

It's not a choice — it's an order of operations.

Build the harness first. Always. Get to where each step is trustworthy: real checks, isolation, memory, tools. Then ask a second question — is "done" something a machine can actually verify? If it is (tests pass, lint clean, the boring stuff), close the loop and walk away. If "done" needs human judgment, keep your hand on it and run the harness interactively.

Loop the things you can verify. Babysit the things you can't. Most people try to loop everything, and then wonder why they spend their evenings firefighting.

The tidy way to hold all this

It's a stack, not a fight. Prompt engineering was about the words. Context engineering was about what the model can see. Harness engineering is about the environment it works in. Loop engineering is about taking your hands off. Each one sits on the last.

The people winning right now aren't the ones who picked loop over harness. They're the ones who built a harness good enough that looping it wasn't terrifying. The model was never the moat — it's everything you build around it, and then, once you trust that, everything you build around that.
