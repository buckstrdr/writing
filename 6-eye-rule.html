The 6-Eye Rule: How I Stop AI Agents Shipping Confident Nonsense

One agent grades its own homework. Two don't share a brain. Six try to prove each other wrong. Here's how many to use, and when.

buckstrdr · 6 min read

A while back an AI agent handed me a result that was completely, confidently wrong. And the part that stuck with me wasn't that it was wrong — it's that it was plausible. Tidy. Well argued. If I'd been in a rush I'd have shipped it.

The "reviewer" I'd pointed at it agreed, too — because I'd asked it the wrong question. I'd asked "does this look right?" Of course it looked right. It was built to look right. That's the whole problem with these things: the confidence is free, and it's identical whether the answer is brilliant or garbage.

The fix isn't a smarter model. It's more eyes — the right kind, pointed the right way, and knowing how many to use for what.

2 eyes: the trap

Two eyes is one agent that both writes the thing and checks it. It marks its own homework. And a model marking its own homework passes it basically every time — it's not lying, it just has no distance from what it made. This sounds too obvious to be worth saying, except it's the default. It's exactly where confident nonsense comes from: nobody with fresh eyes ever looked.

4 eyes: the baseline

Four eyes is the fix everyone reaches for, and it's a good one: the agent that writes it is never the agent that checks it. Different agent — and ideally a different model, because two instances of the same model tend to share the same blind spots and nod along with each other.

The reviewer's only job is to find what the builder missed, and because it didn't write the code, it's got no ego in it. It'll happily throw the builder under the bus. For everyday work — most edits, most features — four eyes is genuinely enough. Don't over-engineer past it for a button colour.

When four isn't enough

For a normal edit, one reviewer is fine. But for a claim that's expensive to be wrong about — a result you're about to build the next thing on top of, a "this works" you're about to ship, a finding you're about to act on — one reviewer is a single point of failure.

It can share the blind spot. It can just be wrong. It can agree too easily because agreeing is the path of least resistance. And here's the nasty bit: the more plausible the nonsense, the more likely a single reviewer waves it through. The dangerous errors are precisely the ones that look right.

6 eyes: make them try to break it

So for the high-stakes stuff I don't use one reviewer. I use several independent ones — and I change the question. Instead of "is this right?" each one gets told: "try to prove this is wrong." Default to refuted. The result only survives if the majority of them fail to break it.

That flip — from confirm to refute — is the entire trick. Three, four, six agents all attacking from different angles, each one defaulting to "this is broken" until proven otherwise. Confident nonsense almost never survives contact with someone actively trying to kill it. Plausible-but-wrong falls apart the moment you stop asking it to reassure you and start asking it to defend itself.

Scale the eyes to the stakes

The obvious objection: six agents attacking everything would be slow and expensive. Correct — so you don't. You don't 6-eye a typo. You match the cost of checking to the cost of being wrong.

Trivial, mechanical stuff — does it compile, do the tests pass, is it lint-clean — gets automated checks and nothing more. Normal work gets four eyes. The expensive-to-be-wrong claims get six. Most people fail at one end or the other: they under-verify everything and ship nonsense, or they over-verify everything and never ship a thing. The rule is just: more stakes, more independent eyes.

It's independence and attitude, not headcount

Here's the bit people get wrong when they hear "six eyes" — they add more reviewers and change nothing else. Six agents that all confirm are worthless. They'll all confirm, confidently, together. Two that genuinely attack are worth more than six that nod.

Two things make an eye count. It has to be independent — a different agent, ideally a different model, without the context that would let it share the builder's blind spot. And it has to be adversarial — told to break it, defaulting to refuted, rewarded for finding the crack, not for being agreeable.

Get those two right and the number is just a dial. You turn it up as the stakes rise.

The result I mentioned at the top?

It survived four separate "looks good to me" reviews. It died in about ten minutes the first time someone actually tried to break it instead of confirm it — the thing that looked like a real signal turned out to be indistinguishable from noise the moment it was properly attacked. Four confirmations, zero value. One genuine attack, problem found. That's the whole case for the 6-eye rule in one story.

A team of AI agents is only ever as trustworthy as its willingness to attack its own conclusions. The model will always sound sure of itself — that's not something you can fix. What you can build is the thing that doesn't care how sure it sounds, and just keeps trying to prove it wrong until it can't. That's the whole game.
