The 6-Eye Rule: How I Stop AI Agents Shipping Confident Nonsense

Three agents confirmed it. Then I told one of them to attack it instead — and only the attack told me anything true.

buckstrdr · 6 min read

A while back a result landed in front of me that looked perfect. A real, exploitable pattern — tidy, well argued, the numbers lined up. Genuinely exciting for about an hour.

I put three agents on it and asked the obvious question: does this hold up? All three said the same thing — "looks good to me." I was one click from building the next thing on top of it.

Then, almost as an afterthought, I went back to one of those same three and changed the question. Not "is this right?" but "try to prove this is wrong." It took about ten minutes to show the whole thing was indistinguishable from random noise.

Three agents had confirmed it. The instant one of them switched to attacking it, it fell apart. Same agents, different question — and only the question that attacked told me anything true. That's the entire case for what I've started calling the 6-eye rule — and it rests on a distinction almost nobody makes: the difference between an agent that confirms and an agent that refutes.

Two eyes: marking your own homework

Start at the bottom. Two eyes is one agent that both does the work and checks it. And a model checking its own work passes it nearly every time — not out of dishonesty, it just has no distance from the thing it made. It's the developer, the reviewer and QA all at once, which really means it's none of them. That's where confident nonsense is born: nobody with fresh eyes ever looked.

Four eyes: the baseline that catches most of it

Four eyes is the obvious upgrade, and a good one — the agent that writes it is never the agent that checks it. Different agent, and ideally a different model, because two instances of the same model tend to share the same blind spots and cheerfully agree with each other.

The reviewer's only job is to find what the builder missed, and since it didn't write the thing, it's got no ego in it. For everyday work — most edits, most features — four eyes is genuinely enough, and I don't reach past it. You don't need a tribunal to approve a button colour.

When four isn't enough

My exciting result had four eyes on it. Four eyes is where it fell down. Because for a claim that's expensive to be wrong about — something you're about to build on, act on, or ship — a single reviewer is a single point of failure. It can share the blind spot. It can just be wrong. It can agree because agreeing is the easy path.

And here's the cruel bit: the more plausible the nonsense, the more likely a lone reviewer waves it through. The dangerous errors are exactly the ones that look right. Mine looked great.

Six eyes: tell them to break it

So for the high-stakes stuff I stopped leaning on one reviewer and put three independent agents on it — that's the six eyes, three fresh pairs — and I changed the question I asked them. Not "is this right?" but "try to prove this is wrong." Default to refuted. The result only survives if the majority of them fail to break it.

That flip — from confirm to refute — is the whole trick. Several agents attacking from different angles, each starting from "this is broken" until proven otherwise. Confident nonsense almost never survives someone actively trying to kill it. Ask it to reassure you and it will, warmly. Ask it to defend itself and it falls apart. Mine fell apart in ten minutes.

Scale the eyes to the stakes

Obvious objection: putting three agents on everything would be slow and expensive. Right — so you don't. I don't 6-eye a typo. You match the cost of checking to the cost of being wrong.

Trivial, mechanical stuff gets automated checks and nothing more — does it compile, do the tests pass. Normal work gets four eyes. The expensive-to-be-wrong claims get six. Most people miss at one end or the other: under-check everything and ship nonsense, or over-check everything and never ship. The rule is just: the more it'd hurt to be wrong, the more independent eyes it earns.

It's independence and attitude, not headcount

Here's what people get wrong the moment they hear "six eyes" — they add more reviewers and change nothing else. Three agents that all confirm are worthless. They'll all confirm, together, confidently. One that genuinely attacks beats three that nod.

Two things make an eye count. It has to be independent — a different agent, ideally a different model, without the shared context that would let it inherit the builder's blind spot. And it has to be adversarial — told to break it, defaulting to refuted, rewarded for finding the crack rather than for being agreeable.

Get those two right and the number is just a dial you turn up as the stakes rise.

Back to that result

The one that looked perfect? Three "looks good to me"s and zero value. One of those same agents, switched to attacking, and the problem was obvious inside ten minutes. If I'd trusted the confirmations, I'd have built a week of work on sand.

A team of AI agents is only ever as trustworthy as its willingness to attack its own conclusions. The model will always sound sure of itself — you can't fix that, and you shouldn't try. What you can build is the thing that doesn't care how sure it sounds, and just keeps trying to prove it wrong until it can't. That's the whole game.
